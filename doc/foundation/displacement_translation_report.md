
# Displacement Translation for Suspension DAQ: Derivation, Calibration, and Rear-Linkage Identification

## Abstract

This report expands Section 2.1 of [overview.md](overview.md) by formalizing the displacement-translation problem for suspension data acquisition. The objective is to explain how raw potentiometer voltage is transformed into wheel travel, why the front and rear suspension models differ, and how the required calibration constants are identified experimentally. Particular attention is given to the rear suspension, where a progressive linkage causes the shock-stroke-to-wheel-travel relationship to become non-linear. A worked rear-linkage example is included to demonstrate the identification procedure. The numerical example is illustrative and should not be treated as a substitute for a measured sweep calibration on the motorcycle under test.

---

## 1. Introduction

In a suspension DAQ system, the primary measured quantity is not wheel travel itself, but an electrical signal generated by a displacement sensor. The purpose of displacement translation is to convert that electrical signal into a physically meaningful kinematic variable that can be used for ride-height analysis, velocity estimation, and damping interpretation.

The transformation is conceptually described by

$$
V_{raw} \rightarrow s \rightarrow W
$$

where:

- $V_{raw}$ is the raw sensor voltage,
- $s$ is the mechanical stroke observed locally at the sensor or damper, and
- $W$ is the true wheel travel relative to a defined chassis datum.

This decomposition is necessary because the sensor is mounted on the suspension assembly rather than at the wheel center. The mapping from local stroke to wheel travel is therefore governed by suspension geometry.

---

## 2. Front Suspension Displacement Translation

For a direct-acting telescopic fork, the mapping from sensor stroke to wheel travel is approximately linear. The overview expresses this as

$$
W_{front} = (V_{raw} \cdot C_{cal}) \cdot \cos(\theta)
$$

This expression is correct in simplified form, but it is useful to separate the electrical calibration from the geometric projection.

### 2.1 Electrical Calibration

A linear potentiometer is modeled as an affine voltage-to-stroke transducer:

$$
s_f = (V_{raw} - V_0) \cdot C_{cal}
$$

where:

- $s_f$ is front sensor stroke in mm,
- $V_0$ is the sensor voltage at the chosen zero reference,
- $C_{cal}$ is the sensor gain in mm/V.

The simplified equation in the overview omits $V_0$, which is acceptable only when offset removal has already been handled during signal conditioning or preprocessing.

### 2.2 Geometric Projection

The fork compresses along its own axis, whereas wheel travel is typically treated as a vertical displacement relative to the chassis. If the fork axis is inclined by angle $\theta$ from vertical, then the vertical component of fork-axis displacement is

$$
W_{front} = s_f \cos(\theta)
$$

Substitution of the calibrated sensor stroke yields the full model:

$$
W_{front} = (V_{raw} - V_0) \cdot C_{cal} \cdot \cos(\theta)
$$

The reasoning behind the front formula is therefore straightforward: the potentiometer converts voltage to local fork-axis displacement, and the fork angle converts fork-axis displacement to vertical wheel travel.

### 2.3 Assumptions Embedded in the Front Model

The front expression is accurate when the following assumptions hold to engineering tolerance:

- The potentiometer output is linear over the operating range.
- The sensor is mounted parallel to fork travel.
- The fork is effectively direct acting, so local sensor stroke is nearly identical to fork compression.
- The angle $\theta$ is treated as constant over the travel range of interest.

The final assumption introduces only a small error in most practical DAQ applications, because changes in fork orientation through the stroke are minor relative to other field-measurement uncertainties.

## 3. Acquisition of the Front Calibration Constant

The front calibration constant $C_{cal}$ must be identified experimentally from a voltage-versus-stroke calibration.

### 3.1 Measurement Procedure

1. Install the front potentiometer in its final mounting geometry.
2. Define a reference position, typically full extension or a known static fork position.
3. Move the fork through several known displacements, preferably in uniform increments.
4. At each increment, record the actual mechanical displacement $s_f$ and the corresponding raw voltage $V_{raw}$.
5. Fit a straight line to the data.

The calibration model is

$$
s_f = mV_{raw} + b
$$

from which:

$$
C_{cal} = m
$$

and

$$
V_0 = -\frac{b}{m}
$$

so that the model can be re-written as

$$
s_f = (V_{raw} - V_0) \cdot C_{cal}
$$

### 3.2 Recommended Practice

- Use at least 5 to 10 calibration points across the usable stroke.
- Record both compression and extension sweeps to reveal hysteresis or friction.
- Allow the mechanism to settle before logging each voltage.
- Inspect residuals after the linear fit.

If the residuals are small and randomly distributed, the linear model is adequate. If the residuals exhibit structure, the usual cause is mounting error, compliance, or friction rather than non-linearity in the potentiometer itself.

## 4. Rear Suspension Displacement Translation

The rear suspension is fundamentally different because the wheel does not act directly on the damper. Instead, the swingarm drives the shock through a linkage whose leverage changes with position. The overview therefore uses a second-order mapping:

$$
W_{rear}(s) = a s^2 + b s + c
$$

where $s$ is shock stroke and $W$ is rear wheel travel.

### 4.1 Mechanical Reasoning Behind the Rear Formula

Define the instantaneous rear motion ratio as

$$
MR(s) = \frac{dW}{ds}
$$

If the linkage ratio were constant, then $MR(s)$ would be constant and the wheel travel relation would be linear:

$$
W = ks + c
$$

However, a progressive linkage is designed specifically so that leverage changes through the stroke. Over a practical operating interval, the motion ratio is often approximated well by a first-order function of shock stroke:

$$
MR(s) = m_1 s + m_0
$$

Integrating with respect to $s$ gives

$$
W(s) = \int (m_1 s + m_0) \, ds = \frac{m_1}{2}s^2 + m_0 s + c
$$

Thus, the quadratic form is not arbitrary. It is the natural consequence of representing the changing linkage leverage with a first-order approximation in shock position.

### 4.2 Interpretation of the Coefficients

For

$$
W_{rear}(s) = a s^2 + b s + c
$$

the coefficients have direct physical meaning:

- $a$ quantifies the variation of motion ratio with stroke,
- $b$ represents the first-order leverage contribution,
- $c$ captures the offset associated with the selected zero datum.

If both shock stroke and wheel travel are referenced to the same fully extended position, then $c$ should be approximately zero. A large non-zero value generally indicates inconsistent datums or biased measurement data.

---

## 5. Identification of the Rear Bike-Specific Constants

The coefficients $a$, $b$, and $c$ are motorcycle-specific and installation-specific. They cannot be inferred reliably from generic geometry or another motorcycle's data. They must be obtained from a physical sweep calibration of the exact rear suspension system under test.

### 5.1 Required Measurements

At a sequence of controlled suspension positions, measure paired values

$$
(s_i, W_i)
$$

where:

- $s_i$ is shock stroke in mm,
- $W_i$ is rear wheel travel in mm at the same suspension position.

### 5.2 Practical Sweep Calibration Procedure

1. Support the motorcycle so the rear wheel can move freely through the full suspension range.
2. Remove the spring from the shock, or otherwise reduce preload sufficiently to permit smooth manual traversal of the linkage.
3. Define a full-extension datum for both wheel travel and shock stroke.
4. Move the rear suspension through the stroke in small, controlled increments.
5. At each increment, record shock stroke and the corresponding rear axle displacement relative to a fixed chassis reference.
6. Repeat the sweep in both directions to assess hysteresis.

Wheel travel is most defensibly measured from axle-center displacement relative to a rigid reference on the chassis. Shock stroke should be taken from the calibrated potentiometer or an independent mechanical scale during the sweep.

### 5.3 Polynomial Identification

The measured data are fit with

$$
W_i \approx a s_i^2 + b s_i + c
$$

In matrix form,

$$
\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}
$$

with

$$
\mathbf{X} =
\begin{bmatrix}
s_1^2 & s_1 & 1 \\
s_2^2 & s_2 & 1 \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\
s_n^2 & s_n & 1
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
\boldsymbol{\beta} =
\begin{bmatrix}
a \\
b \\
c
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The least-squares estimate is

$$
\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^T\mathbf{W}
$$

The resulting coefficient vector $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ contains the bike-specific constants used during DAQ post-processing.

### 5.4 Data-Quality Requirements

- Use at least 8 to 15 measurement points across the stroke.
- Include points near top-out, mid-stroke, and bottom-out.
- Eliminate tire deflection and chassis compliance from the reference setup as far as practical.
- Inspect residuals for systematic shape.

If residuals remain large or structured, then the second-order model is insufficient for that linkage. A higher-order polynomial or lookup-table interpolation may then be justified. Nevertheless, the quadratic form is often a reasonable engineering approximation because it is simple, differentiable, and easy to implement in acquisition software.

## 6. Illustrative Yamaha T7 Rear-Linkage Calibration Example

This section provides a worked numerical example for a T7 rear-linkage calibration workflow. The dataset and coefficients below are illustrative only. They demonstrate the method and plausible linkage behavior, but they are not a substitute for a measured sweep on the specific motorcycle, linkage geometry, ride-height setting, and sensor installation being used.

### 6.1 Example Dataset

Assume the following measured shock-stroke and wheel-travel pairs are obtained during a controlled rear sweep:

| Shock Stroke $s$ (mm) | Rear Wheel Travel $W$ (mm) |
| --- | --- |
| 0 | 0.0 |
| 10 | 40.5 |
| 20 | 78.0 |
| 30 | 112.5 |
| 40 | 144.0 |
| 50 | 172.5 |
| 60 | 198.0 |

These values represent a plausible progressive response in which the effective leverage decreases as the shock approaches deeper compression.

### 6.2 Fitted Polynomial

Applying the least-squares model

$$
W_{rear}(s) = a s^2 + b s + c
$$

to the dataset above gives the illustrative coefficient set

$$
a = -0.015,
\quad
b = 4.20,
\quad
c \approx 0
$$

so that

$$
W_{rear}(s) = -0.015 s^2 + 4.20 s
$$

### 6.3 Interpretation of the Example Fit

The corresponding motion ratio is obtained by differentiation:

$$
MR(s) = \frac{dW}{ds} = 2as + b = -0.03s + 4.20
$$

This means the illustrative linkage begins near

$$
MR(0) = 4.20
$$

and decreases to

$$
MR(60) = 2.40
$$

by 60 mm of shock stroke. That reduction in motion ratio is consistent with a progressive rear suspension, where more wheel force is required per unit damper travel deeper in the stroke.

### 6.4 Use of the Example in DAQ Processing

If the rear potentiometer has already been calibrated to shock stroke,

$$
s_{rear} = (V_{rear} - V_{0,rear}) \cdot C_{rear}
$$

then the complete illustrative rear conversion becomes

$$
W_{rear} = -0.015 s_{rear}^2 + 4.20 s_{rear}
$$

This is the value that would then be differentiated to obtain rear wheel velocity.

### 6.5 Important Limitation

The coefficients in this worked example should be treated only as a methodological demonstration. Actual T7 coefficients must be generated from measured sweep data on the exact bike and setup under investigation.

## 7. Recommended Implementation Model

For practical DAQ processing, the most defensible formulation is to separate electrical calibration from geometry:

### 7.1 Front

$$
W_{front} = (V_{front} - V_{0,front}) \cdot C_{front} \cdot \cos(\theta)
$$

### 7.2 Rear

$$
s_{rear} = (V_{rear} - V_{0,rear}) \cdot C_{rear}
$$

$$
W_{rear} = a s_{rear}^2 + b s_{rear} + c
$$

This decomposition improves traceability because sensor calibration and suspension-geometry calibration can be validated independently.

---

## 8. Consequence for Downstream Analysis

All later kinematic quantities depend on the quality of the displacement translation. In particular, the velocity calculation

$$
v(t) = \frac{dW}{dt}
$$

inherits any scaling, offset, or geometric error present in the displacement model. A poor translation therefore affects more than ride-height interpretation. It directly contaminates damping histograms, compression-rebound balance assessment, and the tuning decisions derived from those plots.

For that reason, front and rear translation models should be validated before any dynamic conclusions are drawn from the acquired ride data.

---

## 9. Conclusion

The front displacement model is approximately linear because the telescopic fork is a direct-acting mechanism and the remaining geometric correction is a simple axis projection. The rear model becomes quadratic because the swingarm-linkage-shock system has a position-dependent leverage ratio, and a first-order approximation of that leverage variation integrates naturally into a second-order wheel-travel relation.

The front calibration constant is obtained from a linear voltage-to-stroke calibration. The rear bike-specific constants are obtained from a physical sweep that measures shock stroke against wheel travel and fits the relation

$$
W_{rear}(s) = a s^2 + b s + c
$$

for the actual motorcycle under test. That calibration step is essential if later velocity-based damping analysis is to be physically meaningful.
